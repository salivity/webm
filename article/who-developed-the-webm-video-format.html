Who Developed the WebM Video Format?

The WebM video format was developed by Google to provide a high-quality, open-source, and royalty-free digital media alternative for use in HTML5 web browsers. First introduced in 2010, the project was launched to address the need for an accessible video format that anyone could use without paying steep licensing fees. This quick overview explores Google’s pivotal role in WebM’s creation, the foundational technologies they acquired to build it, and the collaborative industry support that helped it grow.

Google’s Acquisition and the Birth of WebM

Before WebM, the web was heavily reliant on proprietary video formats like H.264, which required expensive licensing fees for developers and platforms. Google sought to disrupt this landscape by creating a completely free ecosystem.

The breakthrough came in 2010 when Google acquired a company called On2 Technologies. Through this acquisition, Google gained control of a high-quality video codec known as VP8. Google open-sourced the VP8 source code, combined it with the Vorbis audio codec, and wrapped them together in a container based on Matroska (.mkv). This exact combination became the original WebM format.

Collaborative Industry Support

While Google was the primary architect and driving force behind WebM, they did not launch it entirely alone. To ensure the format would succeed across the internet, Google partnered with several major industry players at the time of its release, including:

  • Mozilla: Integrated WebM support into the Firefox browser.
  • Opera: Added native support for the format in their web browser.
  • Hardware Manufacturers: Companies like AMD, ARM, and Nvidia pledged support to build hardware acceleration for WebM, allowing devices to play the videos smoothly without draining battery life.

Evolution to VP9 and AV1

As internet video evolved, Google continued to develop the WebM framework. They later introduced the VP9 video codec, which significantly reduced the file size of high-definition and 4K videos compared to VP8. Today, WebM containers frequently use VP9 or the newer, highly advanced AV1 codec—which Google developed alongside other tech giants under the Alliance for Open Media—solidifying Google’s original vision of a universally accessible web video standard.
